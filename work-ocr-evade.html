OCR-Evade

Design Studio

Independent Practice (ANU)

Medium

P5.js, Letterpress & Laser-cut Acrylic

Year

2026

This project was completed as my third-year graphic design capstone project. It explores the point at which a typeface becomes illegible to a machine while remaining readable to a human. Starting with OCR-B, a typeface originally designed for optical character recognition systems, I used creative coding to progressively distort its letterforms and test how those changes affected machine recognition.

The final outcome combines a hand-bound type specimen book, letterpress printing, laser-cut acrylic letterforms and digital experimentation. By bringing together computational design and traditional printmaking techniques, the project investigates the relationship between typography, machine vision and legibility, questioning whether every form of communication needs to remain accessible to automated systems.

Degradation Stages

Five stages were tested before reducing to three — 0%, 50% and 100%. The difference between adjacent stages was minimal and three stages simplified the letterpress production.

What you read isn’t what the machine reads.

Each specimen is paired with the output of an OCR API run on the same text. The system read only six garbled fragments of the nine-word pangram, and failed to detect the rest at all.

An excerpt from my finished assignment description:

“A typeface originally created for detection is transformed into one designed for evasion. Through corruption and iterative degradation, the typeface becomes increasingly resistant to OCR systems and AI recognition while remaining legible to human audiences.”

Letterpress Plates

Letterforms were laser-engraved into acrylic, then hand-inked and printed. A 3D printed registration guide kept the plates aligned across degradation stages.

Acrylic CMYK Objects

Layered in cyan, magenta and yellow acrylic and displayed slightly offset, the letterforms reference the CMYK print process while introducing another way of disrupting machine vision — the colour separation stays legible to a human viewer but confuses the scan.

Large laser-cut letterforms extend OCR-Evade off the page and into the exhibition space.
